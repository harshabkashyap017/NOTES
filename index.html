Wind Energy

Harnessing the power of moving air to generate electricity!

🌍 What is Wind Energy?

Wind energy is just the energy in moving air. When wind blows, the air molecules are moving — and that movement carries kinetic energy. We capture this energy using wind turbines.

🚴
Think of it like riding a bike downhill — the faster you go, the more energy you have. Same with wind — the faster it blows, the more energy it carries!
375 GW🌍 Global Installed Capacity
50 GW🇮🇳 India's Installed Capacity
For large-scale electricity generation, we use wind farms which can be either offshore (on the sea/water) or onshore (on land). Offshore farms get stronger, steadier winds!

💿 The Actuator Disk Model

To figure out how much energy we can extract from wind, we use the Actuator Disk Model. It imagines the wind turbine as a simple flat disk that slows the wind down.

🤚
Imagine putting your hand out of a car window. Your hand slows down the air passing through it — that's exactly what the actuator disk does to wind!

The average velocity through the disk is:

\[ V_{avg} = \frac{V_i + V_o}{2} \]

V_i = inlet wind speed  |  V_o = outlet wind speed

The mass flow rate of wind through the disk:

\[ \dot{m}_\omega = \rho A_D V_{avg} \]

ρ = density of air (≈1.225 kg/m³)  |  A_D = area of disk  |  V_avg = average velocity

The power output (kinetic energy extracted per second):

\[ P_{out} = \frac{1}{2}\dot{m}_\omega(V_i^2 - V_o^2) \]

This is just the change in kinetic energy of the wind!

Substituting everything and simplifying (let η = V_o/Vᵢ):

\[ P_{out} = \frac{1}{2}P_a(1 + \eta - \eta^2 - \eta^3) \] where \( P_a = \frac{1}{2}\rho A V_i^3 \) is the power available in free stream

🏆 The Betz Limit — Maximum Efficiency of ANY Wind Turbine!

Albert Betz proved in 1919 that no wind turbine can ever extract more than 59.3% of the energy in the wind. This is called the Betz Limit!

🍰
It's like cutting a cake — you can never eat 100% of it cleanly. Some crumbs always escape! The Betz limit says at most ~59.3% of wind's energy can be captured.

How do we find this maximum? We differentiate P_out with respect to η and set it to zero:

\[ \frac{dP_{out}}{d\eta} = \frac{1}{2}P_a(1 - 2\eta - 3\eta^2) = 0 \] Solving: \[ 3\eta^2 + 2\eta - 1 = 0 \implies \eta = \frac{1}{3} \text{ or } \eta = -1 \] We take \(\eta = \frac{1}{3}\) (physically meaningful!)

Plugging η = 1/3 back in:

\[ P_{out}\big|_{max} = \frac{16}{27} P_a \approx \boxed{0.593 \; P_a} \]

This means maximum power extractable = 59.3% of available wind power

59.3%Betz Limit (theoretical max)
η = 1/3V_o/V_i at max efficiency
16/27Exact Betz fraction
This limit is universal — no matter how clever the turbine design, you physically cannot beat 59.3%! Real turbines typically achieve 35–45% because of blade losses, friction, etc.

📐 1D Momentum Theory (Simplified)

This is a more detailed analysis using conservation of momentum. We use control volumes (imagine invisible boxes around the air flow).

Key assumptions:

1
Flow is homogeneous, incompressible, steady
2
No frictional drag on the blades
3
Infinite number of blades (a solid disk!)
4
Uniform thrust over the rotor area
5
A non-rotating disk (no swirl in wake)

Applying Bernoulli's equation for CV-I and CV-II and combining:

\[ P_2 - P_3 = \frac{1}{2}\rho(V_1^2 - V_4^2) \quad \cdots (iv) \]

The Thrust force on the disk:

\[ T = \frac{1}{2}\rho A(V_1^2 - V_4^2) \]

Also: \( T = \rho A_2 V_2 (V_1 - V_4) \)

Assuming V₂ = V₃ (velocity is continuous across disk)

Now we define a = axial induction factor = fractional decrease in velocity:

\[ a = \frac{V_1 - V_2}{V_1} \implies V_2 = V_1(1-a) \quad \cdots (6) \] \[ V_4 = V_1(1 - 2a) \quad \cdots (7) \]

a is also called axial induction factor

What is 'a'? If a = 0, the turbine isn't slowing the wind at all (zero power extracted). If a = 0.5, the wind is stopped to half speed at the disk. If a = 1, the wind stops completely (bad! turbine would stall).

⚙️ Power Coefficient (Cp) — How Efficient is the Turbine?

The Power Coefficient Cp tells us what fraction of available wind power the turbine actually captures:

\[ C_p = \frac{\text{Power of rotor}}{\text{Power in the wind}} = \frac{P_{out}}{P_{available}} \]

After substituting the induction factor formulas, we get:

\[ P_{out} = 2\rho A_2 V_1^3 (1-a)^2 a \] \[ \boxed{C_p = 4a(1-a)^2} \]

This is the power coefficient in terms of axial induction factor a

To maximize Cp, differentiate with respect to a and set to zero:

\[ \frac{dC_p}{da} = 4(1-a)^2 + 4a \cdot 2(1-a)(-1) = 0 \] \[ \Rightarrow a = \frac{1}{3} \text{ (or a = 1, rejected)} \] \[ C_p\big|_{max} = 4 \cdot \frac{1}{3}\left(1 - \frac{1}{3}\right)^2 = \frac{16}{27} \approx 0.593 \]

The Thrust Coefficient CT:

\[ C_T = \frac{\text{Thrust force}}{\text{Dynamic force}} = 4a(1-a) \]

At a = 1/3:   \(C_T = \frac{8}{9} \approx 0.88\)

At a = 0.5:   \(C_T = 1\), \quad C_p = 0.5

Tip Speed Ratio (TSR) — ratio of blade tip speed to free stream wind speed:

\[ TSR = \frac{R\omega}{V_\infty} = \frac{R \cdot 2\pi N}{60 \times V_\infty} \]

R = rotor radius, ω = angular velocity, N = RPM, V∞ = wind speed

📊 Wind Speed Variation with Height

Wind speed is not the same at all heights — it increases as you go higher! We use two main laws to describe this.

① Power Law

\[ V \propto z^\alpha \implies \frac{V_1}{V_2} = \left(\frac{z_1}{z_2}\right)^\alpha \]

z = height  |  α = power index (terrain-dependent)

α valueTerrain type
α = 0.1🌊 Smooth terrain (flat open land)
α ∈ [0.1, 0.2]🌾 Moderately rough terrain
α ∈ [0.25, 0.4]🏙️ Urban / built-up areas
Power law is mainly used for heights of 100–200 m (where most large turbines operate).

② Logarithmic Profile (Log Law)

\[ V(z) = \frac{u^*}{\kappa}\ln\!\left(\frac{z}{z_0}\right) \] where \(u^* = \sqrt{\frac{\tau_0}{\rho}}\) (friction velocity), \(\kappa = 0.4\) (von Kármán constant)

z₀ = roughness length (depends on surface type)

Roughness lengths (z₀) for different surfaces:

Surfacez₀ (mm)
🧊 Smooth ice / mud0.01
🌊 Open sea (calm)0.20
🌊 Wind sea0.50
🌾 Crops50.00
🌳 Forests500
🏘️ Suburban1500
🏙️ Tall buildings3000
Quick Logic: A wind farm in the middle of flat farmland has low roughness (z₀ ≈ 50 mm), so wind speeds up well with height. A turbine in a city surrounded by tall buildings has high roughness (z₀ ≈ 3000 mm) — winds stay choppy and slower. That's why offshore wind farms are more efficient — the sea is very smooth!

🏗️ Wind Farm Design — Four Pillars

🔄 Jensen Wake Model (Top Hat Model)

When a turbine extracts energy from wind, it creates a wake — a region of slower wind behind it. The next turbine in line will get slower wind and produce less power! The Jensen model helps us calculate this.

🏊
Imagine swimming behind someone — you feel the turbulent, disturbed water they leave behind. Wind turbines do the same to air! The second turbine gets the "disturbed" air.

Wake radius at downstream distance x:

\[ R_w = R(1 + 2k\mu) \quad \text{where } \mu = \frac{x}{R} \]

Downstream (wake) velocity:

\[ V_d = U_\infty\left(1 - \frac{1 - \sqrt{1-C_T}}{(1+2k\mu)^2}\right) \]

Velocity deficit:

\[ \frac{\text{deficit}}{U_\infty} = 1 - \frac{V_d}{U_\infty} = \frac{1-\sqrt{1-C_T}}{(1+2k\mu)^2} \]

k ≈ 0.075 (onshore)  |  k ≈ 0.04 (offshore)  |  C_T = thrust coefficient

k can also be expressed as:

\[ k = \frac{0.5}{\ln\!\left(\frac{H_b}{H_0}\right)} \]

H_b = hub height  |  H₀ = roughness height

Wind Farm Efficiency:

\[ \eta_{w.f.} = \frac{\text{Energy generated by all turbines (with wake)}}{\text{Energy generated by all turbines (without wake)}} \]
Example from notes — Square Wind Farm:
Given: Square farm, side = 200 m, HAWT turbines, 3 blades, rotor diameter = 20 m, hub height = 300 m (wait — this looks like 30 m in context), wind speed = 10 m/s steady, k = 0.075

Step 1 — Find wake velocity deficit for T1→T2 (spacing μ = 200/10 = 20): \[ \frac{V_d}{U_\infty} = 1 - \frac{1-\sqrt{1-0.75}}{(1+2\times0.075\times20)^2} = 1 - \frac{1-0.5}{(1+3)^2} = 1 - \frac{0.5}{16} \approx 0.03125\,U_\infty \] So V_d = 0.03125 × 10 = 0.3125 m/s deficit → downstream turbine sees ≈ 9.69 m/s

Step 2 — Calculate efficiencies for square (≈ 95.35%) vs triangle (≈ 98.8%) configuration.
Triangle configuration is more efficient! because turbines are spaced better and the wake affects fewer turbines.

Biomass Energy Sources

Energy from organic matter — plants, animals, and microorganisms!

🌱 What is Biomass?

Biomass is organic carbon-based material from plants, animals, and micro-organisms — grown on land and in water — and their derivatives.

It reacts with oxygen in metabolic processes and during combustion, releasing heat energy.

🌳
Think of a campfire — that's biomass energy! Wood (organic material) burns and releases heat. Biomass energy is just a more sophisticated version of this ancient concept!

Three main methods to get energy from biomass:

1
Thermochemical: Combustion, gasification, pyrolysis
2
Biochemical: Biogas, fermentation to make alcohol fuel (ethanol)
3
Oil Extraction: Biodiesel from plant oils and seeds

🔥 Gasification — Turning Solid Biomass into Gas!

Gasification is the process of converting solid biomass with a limited quantity of air into a combustible gas called producer gas.

🪄
It's like magic — you put in wood chips or agricultural waste, add just a little air, and get a flammable gas out! This gas can run engines and generate electricity.

Producer Gas Composition:

Principal Reactions inside Gasifier:

Pyrolysis:

Thermal decomposition of biomass in the absence of air. Unlike gasification (which has limited air), pyrolysis has zero air.

Types of Gasifiers:

1
Fixed Bed (Downdraft) — gas and biomass flow in same direction (downward)
2
Fixed Bed (Cross-flow) — air enters from side
3
Fluidized Bed — biomass suspended in upward flowing gas (most efficient)
Worked Example from Notes:
A biomass gasifier runs an IC engine (dual fuel, 80% diesel replacement) producing 350 kW. Engine efficiency η = 35%, calorific value of biomass = 16,800 kJ/kg.

Step 1 — Energy needed from producer gas (86% of total = 0.86 × total): \[ \frac{350 \times 10^3}{J/P} = 0.35 \implies J/P\text{ power} = \frac{350\times10^3}{0.35} = 10^6 \approx 1000\text{ kW} \] Step 2 — Energy in producer gas = 800 kW (given η_conversion = 0.75): \[ \dot{m}\times 16800 \times 0.75 = 800 \text{ kW} \] \[ \dot{m} = \frac{800}{16800 \times 0.75} = 0.0634 \text{ kg/s} = 63.4 \text{ g/s} \] Biomass feeding rate ≈ 63.4 g/s

💧 Biogas / Bio-Gas Plant

Biogas is produced when organic waste (like animal dung) is broken down by bacteria without oxygen. The main product is methane (CH₄) — a clean fuel!

Key facts about Biogas Plants:

16,000–25,000Calorific Value of Biogas (kJ/m³)
1 kgDry cattle dung → 1 m³ biogas
40 daysRetention period
1.2 – 6 mDigester diameter range
Sludge = Free Fertiliser! The leftover sludge from the biogas plant contains nitrogen (N), phosphorus (P), and potassium (K) — which are excellent plant fertilisers. So a biogas plant gives you fuel AND fertiliser! 🌱

Digester Sizing Formulas:

Energy available from biogas digester:

\[ E = \eta H_b V_b \] Also: \( E = \eta H_m F_m V_b \)

η = efficiency of burner (~66%)  |  H_b = heating value of biogas (~20 MJ/m³)
V_b = volume of biogas = C·m₀  |  C = biogas per unit day mass of whole input
H_m = CV of methane (~56 MJ/kg)  |  F_m = fraction of methane (~0.7)

Volume of fluid in digester:

\[ V_f = \frac{m_0}{S_m} \]

Volume of digester:

\[ V_d = V_f \cdot t_r \]

S_m = density of dry material in fluid  |  t_r = retention time

Worked Example — Biogas Digester Sizing:
An engine running on biogas produces 2 kW of electric power. Cow dung is used as feed.
Given: CV of biogas = 20,000 kJ/m³, η_gen = 90.1%, η_engine = 3%

Chain: Biogas plant → Generator → Engine → 2 kW output
\[ E_{biogas} \times \eta_{gen} \times \eta_{engine} = 2\text{ kW} \] \[ V_{biogas} = \frac{2}{20000 \times 10^3 \times 0.901 \times 0.03} \approx 3.69 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m}^3/\text{s} \]

For continuous operation (24 hrs × 3600 s): \[ Q_{day} = 0.00037 \times 24 \times 3600 = 31.88 \text{ m}^3/\text{day} \] Mass of dry cow dung needed = 31.88 kg/day
Mass of fresh dung (8% dry content) = 31.88/0.08 = 398.5 kg/day
Volume of digester = 719.1 L/day × 40 days ≈ ~29 m³

🚗 Bio-Diesel — Fuel from Plants!

Biodiesel is produced from edible and non-edible biomass — vegetable oils, yellow grease, cooking oil, animal fats, seeds, nuts, and grains. It's a substitute for conventional diesel!

1. Bio-Ethanol (Alcohol Fuel)

2. Bio-Diesel — Transesterification Process

Biodiesel is made from vegetable oils through a chemical process called transesterification — treating the oil with alcohol (methanol) and a catalyst (NaOH).

Transesterification reaction:

\[ \underbrace{CH_2-OCO-R_1}_{} \] \[ CH-OCO-R_2 + 3CH_3OH \longrightarrow CH-OH + R_2-COOCH_3 \] \[ CH_2-OCO-R_3 \quad\quad\quad\quad CH_2-OH \quad R_3-COOCH_3 \]

Triglyceride (fat/oil) + Methanol → Glycerol + Methyl Esters (Biodiesel)

The methyl esters are the biodiesel! Glycerol is a useful byproduct.

Esterification = combining an organic acid with an alcohol to make an Ester + water
\[ R\text{-}COOH + R'OH \rightleftharpoons R\text{-}COO\text{-}R' + H_2O \] Transesterification = treating an existing ester with a different alcohol to swap the alcohol group. This converts triglycerides (fats) → methyl esters (biodiesel)!
